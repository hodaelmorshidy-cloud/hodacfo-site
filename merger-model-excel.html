A One-Tab Accretion Screen Answers the Wrong Question

Most merger templates give you a single period: combine two net income lines, add synergies, divide by pro-forma shares, print an accretion percentage. That tells you nothing about the thing that actually decides whether a deal works — how the arc behaves once the acquisition debt starts paying down and the intangible amortization runs its course.

This model shows the whole arc. New-share dilution, acquisition-debt interest, per-class intangible amortization and phased synergies all move year by year across five years, on a GAAP basis and again on a cash-EPS basis. The pre-filled reconstruction runs −29.7% in year 1, narrowing to −16.5% by year 5 on GAAP — and −11.2% narrowing to −2.3% on cash EPS. That gap between the two bases is the entire argument in most real deal debates.

No Circular References — By Design

Interest is charged on the beginning balance, so year t depends only on year t−1. The model is genuinely acyclic: no iterative-calculation setting, no circular-reference warning, no recalculation that drifts depending on how many times you press F9. This is also why it survives the trip into Google Sheets intact. It is a deliberate design constraint, disclosed in the file — and it is endorsed accounting practice, since the effective-interest method itself applies the rate to the beginning-of-period balance.

What deal is pre-filled in the model?

Why does the pre-filled deal show as dilutive?

Because the headline figures are on a GAAP basis, after full amortization of the acquired intangibles — a non-cash charge that is also non-deductible in a stock deal with no step-up. On that basis the reconstruction runs −29.7% in year 1, narrowing to −16.5% by year 5 as the debt pays down. On a cash-EPS basis, which adds back after-tax intangible amortization, the same deal runs −11.2% narrowing to −2.3%. CVS itself guided to adjusted-EPS accretion on a forward basis. Teaching that GAAP-versus-cash gap is the point of the model, not a defect in it.

Does the debt schedule have circular references?

No. Interest is charged on the beginning balance, so year t depends only on year t−1 and the model is genuinely acyclic — no iterative-calculation setting, no circular-reference warning, clean recalculation in both Excel and Google Sheets. The schedule still models mandatory note maturities ($3.0bn, $4.0bn and $6.0bn falling inside the five-year window) plus a cash sweep of surplus free cash flow, with total repayment capped at the outstanding balance.

Why does the model show two different consideration figures?

Because both are true and they answer different questions. The model computes roughly $69,969M of total consideration from the offer terms, valuing the stock leg at CVS’s close-date price. CVS’s 10-K reports consideration transferred of $69,766M — the figure usually rounded to $69.8 billion. A single exchange-basis share count cannot reproduce both reported legs exactly, so a difference of about 0.3% is expected. Both are shown side by side and reconciled in a note on the Purchase Price tab, so the two are never confused.

Is this an LBO model?

No, and it is worth being clear about the difference. This is a strategic-acquirer merger model: the debt paydown is corporate deleveraging out of combined free cash flow, and the output is pro-forma EPS. There is no sponsor equity, no IRR or MoIC, and no exit waterfall. If you need those mechanics, that is a different model and a separate product.
