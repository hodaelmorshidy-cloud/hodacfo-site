The Return Came Without Multiple Expansion

The pre-filled deal enters at 10.0x EBITDA and exits at 10.0x. Nothing was re-rated, no cycle was timed, and the exit assumption is not doing any work. The sponsor still turns $660.0M into $1,462.5M — a 2.2x MoIC and a 17.2% dated XIRR — because EBITDA grows from $125.0M to $183.7M and net debt falls from $615.0M to $374.1M. That is the whole engine of a buyout, and it is visible line by line rather than buried in an assumption cell.

Most templates hide the second half of that sentence. They give you one loan and a straight-line paydown, so the deleveraging looks mechanical and the returns look like arithmetic. Real capital structures do not behave that way: the senior amortizes and sweeps, the mezzanine sits untouched until senior is cleared, the PIK note grows the entire time, and the revolver exists to defend a minimum-cash floor you would otherwise breach. This model runs all four.

No Circular References — By Design

Interest is charged on the beginning debt balance, so year t depends only on year t−1. The model is genuinely acyclic: no iterative-calculation setting, no circular-reference warning, no recalculation that drifts depending on how many times you press F9. This is also why it survives the trip into Google Sheets intact. It is a deliberate design constraint, disclosed in the file — and it matches the effective-interest method, which applies the rate to the beginning-of-period balance.

The Waterfall, In Priority Order

Free cash flow does not simply “pay down debt”. In this model it pays cash interest on senior, mezzanine and the revolver; PIK interest accretes to principal instead of being paid; D&A and the PIK charge are added back; capex and the change in working capital come out. What is left funds mandatory senior amortization first, then repays any revolver drawn against the minimum-cash floor, then any dividend recap you switch on, and only then sweeps — 100% of remaining cash, into senior until it is cleared, then mezzanine. In the pre-filled deal that sweep is what takes senior from $375.0M to $86.5M in five years while mezzanine never moves.

Common Questions

Why does total debt fall by less than the amount repaid?

Because one tranche is growing while the others shrink. In the pre-filled deal the senior term loan is repaid from $375.0M to $86.5M — $288.5M of amortization and cash sweep — while the PIK note accretes from $62.5M to $110.1M, because PIK interest is paid in kind and capitalized into principal rather than settled in cash. Mezzanine is untouched at $187.5M until senior is cleared. Net of the $47.6M of PIK accretion, the stack falls $240.9M, not $288.5M. A single-tranche template cannot show this, because it has nothing to accrete.

Does the model have circular references?

No. Interest is charged on the beginning debt balance, so year t depends only on year t−1 and the model is genuinely acyclic. That means no iterative-calculation setting, no circular-reference warning, and clean recalculation in both Excel and Google Sheets. The waterfall still runs in full: cash interest, PIK accretion, mandatory senior amortization, a revolver draw against the minimum-cash floor, optional dividend recaps, and then a 100% cash sweep in order of priority — revolver, then senior, then mezzanine.

What returns does the pre-filled deal produce?

The sample target, Northwind Components Inc., is fictional. Entry is at 10.0x EBITDA of $125.0M for an enterprise value of $1,250.0M, funded with $625.0M of debt across three tranches and $660.0M of sponsor equity. Over a five-year hold, EBITDA grows to $183.7M and net debt falls to $374.1M. Exiting at the same 10.0x multiple gives exit equity of $1,462.5M — a 2.2x MoIC, a 17.3% annualized IRR and a 17.2% dated XIRR. Note that the return comes with zero multiple expansion: entry and exit are both 10.0x, so every dollar is earned from EBITDA growth and deleveraging.
