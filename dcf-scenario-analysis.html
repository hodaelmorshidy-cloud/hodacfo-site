DCF Scenario Analysis

How to build bear, base, and bull cases in a DCF valuation model—and why running all three separates institutional analysis from guesswork. Microsoft pre-loaded example with real numbers.

By Hoda Elmorshidy • Fractional CFO • Financial Modeling • Quantitative Analysis

A DCF valuation produces a single number—but that number is only as reliable as the assumptions that feed it. Change the revenue growth rate by 1%, or adjust the terminal growth rate by 0.5%, and the implied share price moves by tens or even hundreds of dollars. This is why institutional investors never rely on a single-point DCF output. They build three scenarios: bear, base, and bull.

Base Case: The Anchor

The base case represents your best estimate of the company's future performance based on current trends, management guidance, and reasonable industry growth expectations. It is not conservative and it is not aggressive—it is the scenario you would defend in an investment committee as the most likely outcome. For Microsoft, the base case assumes continued Azure and AI-driven growth at 12.9% revenue CAGR (stepping from 18% in Year 1 to 8% by Year 10), operating margin reaching 45.5% by Year 5, and capital returns through buybacks. The result: $369.74 implied share price, which suggests Microsoft is slightly overvalued at its $407.77 market price.

Bear Case: The Stress Test

The bear case answers the question: what if the optimistic thesis is wrong? It does not assume a catastrophe. It assumes a realistic but adverse set of conditions—slower growth, margin pressure, higher discount rates. For Microsoft, this might include decelerating cloud adoption, increased competition in AI, or regulatory headwinds. The bear case reduces base case revenue growth by 4.5 percentage points each year, with a 2.0pp compression in operating margin. It is not the worst-case scenario—it is the low end of a reasonable range. At $248, the market is pricing in some downside relative to base expectations.

Bull Case: The Upside

The bull case captures the outcome if the company executes at the top of its potential. This is not a fantasy scenario—it is grounded in what the company has achieved historically during strong cycles. For Microsoft, the bull case assumes AI monetization accelerates faster than expected, operating leverage drives margin to 2.0pp above the base case (47.5% by Year 5), and capital efficiency improves. At $549, the bull case implies approximately 35% upside from the market price, which is well within the range of what growth-oriented investors would consider reasonable for a business of Microsoft's quality.

Scenario Analysis vs Sensitivity Analysis

These two concepts are frequently confused. Scenario analysis changes multiple assumptions simultaneously to create a coherent alternate reality (e.g., high growth + high margins for the bull case — in this model, WACC is held constant across all three scenarios so the spread isolates operating assumptions; the discount rate is stressed separately in the sensitivity table). Sensitivity analysis changes one assumption at a time to isolate its marginal impact (e.g., how does implied share price change if WACC moves by 0.25%?). Both are essential. The Institutional-Grade DCF Model includes a full sensitivity table showing implied share price across a WACC versus terminal growth rate grid, plus the three integrated scenarios.

Why 3 Scenarios Matter

A single-point DCF output gives you false precision. Three scenarios give you a range. When you present a valuation range of $248–$549 with a base case of $369.74 against a market price of $407.77, you are showing discipline. You are not saying "I know the exact value." You are saying "here is my framework, here are my assumptions, and here is the range of outcomes those assumptions produce." That is what institutional investors pay for.

How to Build 3 Scenarios in Excel

Building a multi-scenario DCF model in Excel requires a deliberate structural choice. The cleanest approach is a single projection engine with input cells that reference a scenario selector. Each assumption (revenue growth, operating margin, capex, WACC, terminal growth rate) has three values—one per scenario. A dropdown or switch lets you toggle between them, and every output recalculates instantly. This approach is more maintainable than building three separate models, and it prevents the errors that come from inconsistent assumptions across scenarios.
